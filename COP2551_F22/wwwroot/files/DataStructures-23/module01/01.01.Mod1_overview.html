Let's Review!

This week, we're going to dive into the beginning of our textbook. But don't be misled. We won't be moving sequentially through the book.

Most professional developers have at least some experience with multiple languages. It is not unusual to work for months and even years at a time with a team of developers using the a single language and relatively static development environment. Often programmers will acquire a comfort level and even an affinity for one language out of familiarity. Having an exposure to- and working knowledge of multiple programming languages will make you a better engineer.

The pre-requisites of this course imply a certain level of experience with at least a couple of programming languages. Most of the languages you have encountered will share certain things in common, and most of the programs and projects you have tackled, shared common logic, approaches, and patterns. Conditional statements, looping, assignments, string manipulation, and math operations. The syntax may vary, but the similarities should be very apparent by now.

For most of you, C++ is not a new language. If this is a new experience, take the time to browse through the first few chapters to come up to speed, or if it has been a while, consider trying some of the examples to refresh your memory. I believe you will be comfortable enough very quickly.

Data-centric

So, given that you have the solid foundation of those patterns and practices, in this course we'll be taking a closer look at the data structures, and how to optimize code. These topics are generally not language-specific, but C++ is a good platform to do that investigation.

The goals for this module:

  • Install and configure Visual Studio (Community Edition)
  • Review primitive variable types and memory usage
