  3. In the field of software engineering, from time-to-time, you may be invited to technical interviews. You may be asked for simple definitions for common concepts, perhaps the 4 pillars of object-oriented programming, or the phases of the software development life cycle. You can probably think of other common concepts that you've learned in classes. For these, your instructor, or textbook, or the Google(!) offered a working definition and it was enough to satisfy the question at the time.
    It's a good idea to take some of these basic concepts, maybe find a few example definitions, and then define them in your own words, and refine and practice them, so that they're ready for interview responses. For example, you know what a variable is. Our textbook says Variables represent storage locations in the computer's memory.
    My definition is:
    A variable is a named storage location in the computer's memory where a value may be stored and accessed later. The value of a variable can be changed (may "vary") throughout the execution of a program.

    (20 points) In a paragraph or two, discuss:
    Do you agree with this definition? Is it more or less clear, concise, or correct than what you can find in the textbook or a web search? Are there other terms or concepts that you have learned that you might want to revisit and commit to memory before an interview? (encapsulation, polymorphism, abstraction, test-driven development, frameworks, ...)
