An agile approach

A plan is a point of departure

I race sailboats. I've raced many different classes of highly competitive boats from small dinghies to large keelboats with crews from one or two to fifteen to twenty. Success in racing requires the same fundamental truths as success in any other sport, development project, or business venture. Preparation, planning and execution. And success teaches us that these cannot be separated.

For us, success starts long before the starting line. Preparation means the equipment is ready: correctly selected, installed, and maintained. It means that the team is well-selected and compatible with the objective and members are individually prepared for the task. It means understanding the environment, including knowing what is, and isn't in the local charts and weather reports. It includes tracking local and regional weather observations and forecasts and tide schedules and current patterns for changes over time . All of this adds up to showing up the day of a race with a well-planned and communicated strategy, including knowing what to do when our well-planned and communicated strategy goes right out the window because of changes to what we expected during our planning.

Strategy
is being prepared for excellent performance in expected conditions.
Tactics
is knowing how to deal with the reality that conditions and situations change.

It is good to plan.

It is best to know how to adjust your plan when what you thought you knew doesn't happen.

In this module, we'll revisit what planning means in software engineering, and how we, as an industry, went so wrong.

 

Following a plan

No plan survives first contact with the enemy.

- Helmuth von Moltke more

We started out this course talking about the SDLC, and the different phases of the life cycle, how requirements analysis leads to design. And an approved design should be completed before implementation. And that a solution should be fully tested and delivered to the customer in a neat, handoff, with a transition to maintenance and support activities.

It's a great idea.

Where it usually fails is in the application (of that idea).

First, the notion that there will be an end to one phase leading to the beginning of the next, usually needs to be an ongoing, cooperative conversation rather than a clean handoff.

Next, is that it needs to be recognized that planning is based on assumptions and predictions, which can vary in their accuracy.

Manifesto for Agile Software Development

Twenty years ago, a rift between engineering and project management teams was growing silently across the industry.

Process scientists had studied and designed and redesigned approaches to completing projects which followed measurable and predictable formulae, and the world had subscribed to it. We, as industry professionals would decompose project objectives into measurable granula requirements, which could, in theory, be forecast and with estimated with some degree of certainty, and these small efforts would then be rolled up and sequenced with entry and exit criteria and progress could be monitored, measured, and reported against that plan and overall project success could be ensured.

A fundamental flaw was that project success was measured against that imperfect plan, rather then the project objectives themselves.

At this time, a group of successful engineers organized a meeting to discuss these frustrations. They did not provide solutions. Rather, they defined the developed a set of principles which became revolutionary.

Process and design documents and meetings are all important parts of product development, but this group formally authored a set of ideas that emphasized that these documents and artifacts are secondary products to support the solution development effort, not the products themselves.

They called this document their Manifesto for Agile Software Development .

We are uncovering better ways of developing
software by doing it and helping others do it.
Through this work we have come to value:

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
 Working software over comprehensive documentation
 Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
 Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on
the right, we value the items on the left more.

What needs to change?

A real world example

About the time this was taking place, I was working on a project that had run into delays and execution problems in the first contract year. Our company, a large contrating company, was successful and took pride in excellence in program execution. When something was wrong, we took action and fixed it. Still, with a large contract and a larger customer, There are a lot of formally defined gates and processes to be followed. We dilligently authored and followed processes and were the model project team, setting examples for others to follow. At the end of the second year, the customer was very happy, we were back on schedule, showing a profit for the company and proudly looking forward to delivering a quality product.

The frustration was that by the time design, development, test, training and deployment were complete, the program was in its 4th year. But the pre-contract award proposal activity had taken about 9 months. The original Operational Concept Document (OCD) on which our requirements had been based was written and approved about 2 years prior to that. So from recognizing the user need to final delivery, almost 7 years had passed.

This is not an unusual scenario. 7 years is a lifetime in technology, but our industry had been perpetuating this pattern. All of the processes were carefully developed to ensure best practices, but feeding the machine had quietly become more important than meeting customer objectives within a reasonable time.

 

If you read the simple document that these engineers called their Manifesto, you'll recognize that these design documents and meetings and reviews all certainly had value, but these needed to be used for 2-way communication and reaching understanding, not viewed as the delivery objectives themselves.

while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more

 

As I said before, in this course, we began with a discussion of the SDLC and a discussion about some of the artifacts that are produced in many large projects. It is important to recognize that there is a correct way to do these, and that they serve a purpose, but what we are learning is that if they are always labeled DRAFT that might be ok.

 

At the time this Manifesto was written, many thought it had some good ideas, but there were also those who thought these engineers were anarchists and it took a very long time for anything to change.

Remember that nowhere in this ideal of becoming more agile in software development processes, did this group make any suggestions about how this should be done.

Some were proponents of eXtreme Programming (XP), Kanban and time-boxes had been around for a while already, as had scrum. But for large companies and large customers, these ideas were not considered viable. They certainly didn't fit the predictable, repeatable measures of success they were used to.

As these ideals began to be more mainstream there were a lot of discussions and efforts for projects to become more lean and agile and able to adjust more quickly to changes in requirements and directions.

In my own opinion and observations, the one thing that distinguishes many of these practices with traditional processes is the more frequent feedback loop. Traditionally, review events were often week- or two-week long events attended by many participants. The presentations were polished finalized documents intended for official sign-off. These meetings became rehearsed presenations because schedule setbacks could be costly. In contrast, instead of these large, infrequent formal meetings, teams trying to be more agile, are meeting frequently and presenting current-state, implementations. Instead of formal finalized documents, just-enough designs are presented. Just enough to get started. Just enough to build the next feature or the next view.

Instead of waiting for a grand-unveiling, the customer-stakeholders see small incremental changes. Teams are smaller, and decisions are faster.

Agile is an adjective, not a noun!

Organizations don't "Do Agile" they try to become more agile.
