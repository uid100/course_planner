Product Testing

Testing activities should take place throughout the lifecycle of a project. From the start of requirements gathering, considerations for testability, and test planning need to be considered. As the product matures, products and artifacts are tested against the decomposed requirements as well as the customer's originating requirements and objectives.

For project management, primary consideration needs to be given to the cost of defect correction over the life cycle of the product. The cost of detecting and fixing errors increases substantially as the project advances from requirements through design through implementation and on to delivery and maintenance.

For this reason, even design artifacts are reviewed by the test team for correctness and measured against requirements.

It's less expensive to correct a design before it has been implemented, and less expensive to fix a code method before it has been integrated. If the product reaches acceptance testing, substantial costs are likely incurred to correct even minor defects.

Escapes or Escaped Defects are defects (bugs) that are identified after a product release.

Costs of escapes may go beyond the cost of correcting them. Repairing a company's brand reputation can be crippling.

Unit Testing

An important part of development should include unit testing.

Unit testing is a software testing technique in which individual units or components of a software application are tested in isolation from the rest of the application. The purpose of unit testing is to validate that each unit or component of the software is functioning correctly and to identify any defects or errors in the code.

A unit in this context typically refers to a single function, method, or class. The unit is tested by providing it with inputs and evaluating its outputs to verify that it produces the expected result. The test cases are typically automated, allowing for efficient and consistent testing.

How big a unit or compenent needs to be is subjective, but usually includes every method in a class, including the constructor.

Developers write unit tests as they are developing and the result is a robust library of tests which can be autonomously and automatically run throughout the development process.

Writing and continuously running these tests are low cost insurance that later corrections don't result in errors to previously working code.

Acceptance Testing

Near product delivery, final testing may be known as different names and may take on different appearances.

Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT) involves preparing and repetitively running through a set of formal tests intended to demonstrate that requirements have been met. This set of testing is usually done internally to prepare for Customer Acceptance Testign (CAT), which is often performed or witnessed by the customer or a representative.

Validation and Verification (V & V)

Measurement against requirements can also be categorized with different focus and strategies. The decomposed, detailed requirements are individually Validated to show that they are passing the focused tests built specifically to their detail, and the system operation is also Verified either in the actual or a simulation of the intended environment under normal operating conditions.

Another way of considering this:

  • Validation - Did we build it right?
  • Verification - Did we build the right thing?

 

Test Readiness Review

In most product deliveries, prior to formal acceptance testing, a product team will conduct a Test Readiness Review (TRR). As the name suggests, this process ensures that the software product is ready for testing, and identifies any potential issues that may affect the quality of the testing.

For this review, the requirements, as well as the test plan and test environment are reviewed for readiness, including hardware, software, and network resources. The test data is assembled and checked for completeness, the test cases and scripts, and acceptance criteria should have been reviewed to ensure they cover the requirements to be tested.

The ojective of the review, is a risk assessment to ensure that everything is prepared.

Requirements Traceability Matrix

A requirements traceability matrix (RTM) is a document that is used to track and manage the relationship between requirements and other aspects of a software development project. The purpose of an RTM is to ensure that all requirements are met, and to provide traceability from the requirements to other elements of the project, such as design, development, testing, and deployment.

An RTM typically consists of a table that lists the requirements and associated information, such as the requirement ID, description, priority, and status. The matrix also includes columns for tracing the requirement to other elements of the project, such as test cases, design documents, and code modules. The matrix may include additional information, such as the name of the person responsible for the requirement, the date the requirement was added or changed, and any comments or notes related to the requirement.

The RTM helps ensure that all requirements are addressed and that they are properly tested and validated. It also provides a way to track changes to requirements throughout the project, helping to ensure that the project stays on track and that all stakeholders are aware of the project status.

A Requirements Traceability Matrix is a document that helps to ensure that all requirements are properly addressed.

 

Project Closeout

By now, you have seen the different phases of the SDLC and may be wondering or realizing that that Testing and even product delivery is not the end of the life cycle. In most cases, product handoff will lead to a period of maintenance and support and often continued development for subsequent product feature releases and updates. Often, this may be defined as a new project.

In any case, it still defines the ending of one phase of development and perhaps the start of another, and as you have seen, is usually accompanied by another review.

Even in small iterations such as sprints, there are often two separate reviews, the review with the stakeholders, to measure completeness against planned delivery, and an internal retrospective which captures lessons learned for process improvement. When conducted with this in mind, these reviews serve to improve the team, the processes, and the business unit for the next effort. There are business considerations and activities associated with the project closeout, but don't overlook the opportunity to capture ideas and thoughts from the team before they are released and reassigned to another project.
