Process Review

 

Delivering a software product involves a series of processes that ensure that the software is developed, tested, deployed, and maintained successfully.

    Key processes include:
  • Requirements gathering and analysis
  • Design and architecture
  • Implementation and coding
  • Testing
  • Deployment
  • Maintenance and support

The scope and methodology of each process will vary between projects and products. Our intent was not to study all that is involved with each but to introduce the concepts and to recognize how they rely and build on each other and why.

Processes will be tailored not only dependent on the project scope, but also based on the size and experience of the team. A large team with many moving parts will almost certainly require more carefully planned processes, than a team of one or two. A team which has history of successful project deliveries will have collected experiences and process improvements to streamline steps over teams that are finding their way for the first several times.

Project Lessons Learned

    Answer each of the following 4 questions.
  • What did you do right? When looking back over a project, it is easy to focus on the unfinished items on the to-do list or the mistakes which led to restarts and redo-s. For this, consider the pieces that came together well either because of planning, experience, inspiration or something else.
  • What did you do wrong? This is sometimes the most obvious place to look for lessons learned and things to be avoided in the future.
  • Where were you lucky? This is one of my favorites. This is often left off of a project review, but it's one of the most important. The inclination is to celebrate these things, but don't. Lucky is often due to risks which may or may not have been identified which didn't materialize. This is where important lessons to be learned are often overlooked.
  • What would you change / what do you know now that you wish you knew "then"? There are always more projects either already in progress or waiting to be started. The more tasks that you complete and projects you are involved with, the more value you will bring your project teams. One suggestion that I make to all (and especially new) engineers and developers is to keep a notebook. Write down every assignment you get and make a note in the margin how long you estimate the task will take. When it is finished mark how long it took. Over time, the difference between those numbers should get closer together. This is an important skill to learn. At first, you do not need to share this with anyone but soon, you will be asked to estimate your work. It's a good way to get started.
