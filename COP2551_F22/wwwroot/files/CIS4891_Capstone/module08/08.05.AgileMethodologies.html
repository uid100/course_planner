Read between the lines and consider the ideas discussed.

Process and tools are essential for creating repeatable, cost-effective products. Without business support, most software and system development would never happen. Finding ways to be more effecient are essential to that objective, until those processes interfere with doing those things.

Sometimes, comprehensive documentation is itself the customer's objective and the deliverable product. Accurate design documentation and records will also go a long way to aiding engineer and end-user training and product maintenance and support. But more often, design artifacts should approximate the final product. Used as a communications tool to guide but not steer development.

Contracts versus collaboration. I've experienced both sides of this, and it can be a sensitive topic. In my opinion, the only beneficial path through this is to ensure that business interests between customer and supplier are well-represented through the development process.

Change is constant. As you have heard, at the beginning of a project, you can't know all that you don't know. Response to change, more than anything else, is the goal of agile development.

Methodologies

The departure from the traditional waterfall pattern of the SDLC is not as extreme as it seems. We still need an emphasis on Requirements capture, decomposition, and management. Design artifacts are still a primary means of communication and preliminary development. Implementation and test still are dependent on one another and all of these go hand-in-hand with maintenance and support activities. The lessons we are learning probably have more to do with the blurring of the boundaries between these activities

The message from the video, is not that these methodologies are bad. But rather that they need to be tailored to meet objectives. Large, formal, multi-team integration programs should have much different process requirements than smaller, simpler, fast-track projects.

Methodologies, guidelines, principles of practice are a good starting point. When you learn to play a musical instrument, you may start with playing scales, and you practice them until you are proficient, but noone expects the musician to be limited to playing scales forever. It is a skill which is a means to an end. Software development methodologies should also be followed closely to learn their value, and once the why and how are understood, they should be tailored to fit the needs of the project and organization.

Extreme Programming (XP)

One of the more non-traditional approaches, XP is intended to accelerate product delivery by minimizing design in favor of figuring it out along the way.

Extreme Programming (XP) is a software development methodology that emphasizes teamwork, collaboration, customer satisfaction, and high-quality software development. XP is one of the Agile software development methodologies and is intended to improve software quality and responsiveness to changing customer requirements.

XP is based on four core values: communication, simplicity, feedback, and courage. These values guide the XP team in their approach to software development. XP has several practices that support these values, including:

  • Planning: XP emphasizes continuous planning and frequent delivery of working software.
  • Testing: XP advocates for continuous automated testing to ensure the quality of the software being developed.
  • Pair Programming: In XP, developers work in pairs, sharing a single computer to complete programming tasks.
  • Continuous Integration: XP emphasizes frequent integration of code changes into a shared code repository.
  • Refactoring: XP encourages continuous refactoring to improve the quality of the codebase.
  • Simple Design: XP promotes a simple and clean design approach that focuses on the essential features needed by the customer.
  • Collective Ownership: XP encourages collective ownership of the codebase, where all team members can make changes and have a stake in the success of the project.

Overall, XP is designed to help development teams quickly deliver high-quality software that meets customer needs through a collaborative and iterative approach to development.

In some ways, XP is one of the least repeatable, and least understood, and often least accepted methodologies.

Kanban

Kanban is a Japanese term that loosely translated means visual cards. The practice is in fact, built around a bulletin board with task cards. Members of the team select cards and complete tasks. A product lead ensures that tasks are prioritized and getting accomplished to meet the project goals.

Kanban is a software development methodology that is based on the principles of Lean manufacturing, which is focused on reducing waste in the form of unnecessary action and cost. Kanban originated in the manufacturing industry, where it was used to improve production efficiency by managing the flow of work.

In software development, Kanban is a visual process management system that helps teams manage and prioritize their work. It is based on the concept of a Kanban board, which is a visual representation of the work that needs to be done, work in progress, and work that has been completed. The Kanban board is typically divided into columns that represent different stages of the development process.

Kanban emphasizes continuous delivery and encourages teams to work in small, manageable batches. Work items are pulled from a backlog and added to the Kanban board as the team is ready to work on them. As work progresses, team members move work items across the board, from one column to the next, until the work is completed.

One of the key principles of Kanban is to limit work in progress (WIP) to avoid overloading the team and to ensure that work is completed efficiently. The number of work items that can be in progress at any given time is typically limited to a small number to help the team stay focused and ensure that work is completed quickly.

Scrum

While Scrum and Kanban do share similarities, scrum is built around timebox boundaries called sprints. The team agrees on what can be accomplished in these fixed schedule increments and work proceeds, as a team, to complete the set of tasks. Often the team will arrange the sprint goals in a similar way to the Kanban board but the day-to-day operational cadence and stakeholder involvement is usually slightly different.
