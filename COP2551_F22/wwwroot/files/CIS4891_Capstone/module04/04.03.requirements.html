Defining Requirements

In the last module, we talked about generating User-Stories from thoughtful conversations with the intended user or customer, ideally in the context of the target operating environment. From these User Stories, a solution can be proposed and system Use-Cases developed.

This usually leads to generation of formal requirements.

Large, formal projects can typically generate hundreds of requirements. These formal requirements ensure that projects meet as well as stay within scope the scope of contracted work. In simple terms, to make sure the customer gets what they're paying for, and so that the development team gets paid for what they deliver.

So for most projects, requirements are a very important part of the development process.

It is not unusual for large contracted projects to have teams of engineers working on requirements management. These engineers try to ensure that the customer's objectives and the designed solution match and that the understood objectives are clearly communicated between the customer and the development team. This requires considerable back and forth communications to ensure that the customer knows what they are ordering and that the development team stays within those bounds.

There are formal document templates for writing standard requirements specification documents, but we are only going to focus on a very small piece of that, writing and recognizing good requirements.

    A good requirement definition should be:
  • Clear: It should be well-defined, easy to understand and free of ambiguity.
  • Complete: It should include all necessary information, including constraints and assumptions, to fully describe the requirement.
  • Consistent: It should not contradict other requirements and should align with the overall project goals and objectives.
  • Traceable: It should be traceable to the source of the requirement, such as customer needs, business goals, and regulatory requirements.
  • Verifiable: It should be testable, measurable and have clear acceptance criteria.
  • Relevant: It should be directly related to the project and necessary for its successful completion.
  • Prioritized: Requirements should be prioritized based on their importance, feasibility, and dependencies.

By having these characteristics, a requirement definition can help ensure that the final product meets the needs and expectations of stakeholders and supports the success of the project.
