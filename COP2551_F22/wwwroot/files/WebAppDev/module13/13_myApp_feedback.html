Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The SDLC is an important concept in Software Engineering. It is a logicical, structured, phased process approach to software development.

Read that again.

  • Logical
  • Structured
  • Phased
  • Process

This is well established and widely accepted.

Take a moment and launch a web search for "SDLC." The results may surprise you. For such a fundamental concept in our industry, it is a little confusing (to me) that there is not consensus on just what these phases are or even how many steps, stages, phases there are!

What is agreed is that the process (life cycle) begins with defining and planning the product and process, followed by designing the solution, then implementation, testing, and maintaining. Each of these activities is worthy of deeper investigation on their own and perspectives may change some from project to project, and the boundaries between the activities may blur and shift from project to project.

 

Waterfall

For many years the "gold standard" has been to follow a strict sequential process for all large, formal engineering processes. Just as water flows downhill in only one direction between vertical changes, the waterfall engineering process is intended to be a single direction, sequence of steps.

This is characterized by clear start and end conditions for each phase of development. Usually there is a formal review of defined artifacts which represent formal entry and exit criteria for each stage.

A Planning Review would ensure that a project was correctly resourced and authorize funding and a kickoff for the next steps.

A Requirements review with customer and other stakeholders would provide an initial agreement of what the product's goals and objectives.

The design team (usually system engineers) would work to describe an acceptable design from the formal requirements and may have a series of Preliminary Design Reviews and a Final Design Review.

Not until this final design is reviewed and approved would the developers begin to implement the solution. This implementation of course does include interim unit and integration testing, but concludes before final formal acceptance testing.

The support and maintenance phase also has many activities associated with it.

In a perfect world, the waterfall process is an excellent model to follow and manage. In reality, there are significant challenges with it. It is difficult to predict all of the details that will be involved when constructing a complex system. Often this leads to ratcheting back to earlier phases to make adjustments.

If a requirement needs to be revisited, fixed, or clarified during the implementation phase, for instance, the design may need to be reviewed and updated, and other implications on the system may need to be considered. This occasional interruption of the single directional process flow is one of the biggest challenges of the waterfall process.

 

Agile (Scrum/Kanban/XP)

In the software engineering world, there is still some confusion about the term agile. It is a movement which was launched by a conference of software engineers looking for ways to improve the software process. At the end of their discussion, they concluded that product (good code) should be prioritized over process (artifacts). Simply put, they respected and appreciated the logical activities: concept definition, requirements, design, implementation, test, and maintenance, but recommended that, as an industry, there needed to be a reasonable and logical approach to handling changes, rather than the rigid, single-direction waterfall model.

That's it. They published their recommendations, but did not include any suggestions for how to do this.

As a result of this call for a more Agile approach, several alternatives were proposed. Most notably:

  • Kanban
  • Scrum
  • eXtreme Programming (XP)

 

Kanban

Kanban is a Japanese term which means (approximately) "bulletin board" or "sign board." The concept is to decompose a project into small tasks and post them in a shared location. Team members select from the tasks (cards) on the todo list, one at a time. The task (card) visibly will move from the todo list to a list indicating that it is assigned and actively being worked. The team member will then work the task to completion and move it to the next column to indicate it is complete and (usually) ready for testing, and will retrieve another task (card).

The concept was made popular from documented processes in Japanese automobile manufacturing, designed to optimize productivity and eliminating activities seen as wasteful. "The Toyota Way"

While team members are cycling through the tasks on the todo list and moving them across the board, a product owner (usually) will be managing the task backlog and providing new tasks to the board, opening new tasks, and reviewing the completed tasks and either closing them or reopening them if it is needed.

If you are-, or someone you know is- a list-maker, you will appreciate the value of this approach. The key points are to keep an acheivable but not overwhelming list of tasks available, and having a team disciplined to maintain focus on 1 (maybe 2) task(s) at a time.

 

Scrum

The term Scrum comes from the game of rugby and the manner of play which involves coordinated effort of a formation of players. This idea that a team can form and manage themselves is central to scrum approach to development. In this model, a fixed amount of time is defined, usually about 2 weeks, and as a team, there is a set amount of work that is to be accomplished.

Within each defined time period, called "sprints," the team works together to design, implement, and test the associated goals for the sprint. The goal of each sprint is to have a testable product which will evolve toward the final objective with each successive sprint.

During the sprint, there are regular checkpoints to communicate direction and progress to ensure the team and stakeholders remain on a correct course.

 

Extreme Programming (XP)

Some of the principles of XP include Pair Programming and parallel testing. With Pair Programming, engineers work collaboratively, sharing ideas and actively communicating and peer reviewing and testing production as the product is being developed. This approach relies on following industry best practices and requires considerable trust and clear communication to ensure design direction is consistent with customer intent.
