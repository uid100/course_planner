So what can you expect?

There are 15 weeks in the term. We're going to walk through one of Tony Gaddis' Starting out with... textbooks.

 

If you don't already have it, find it quick. If you got a great deal on an older edition, you may be ok for at least a few weeks. We're going to be using Microsoft Visual Studio. The current release is '2022.' The Community Edition is a free download. It will take some time and not an insignificant amount of storage space on your computer, but it is the industry standard and you will need this to be successful.  I have a current release, and will be installing any updates this weekend. We're not working on legacy code, so there's no reason not to use the latest and find any sticking points as we go along. (Real-world stuff!). 

Our textbook will reference different versions of the tool, and we may encounter some differences and even some challenges along the way, but that really is what we sometimes face as code developers. When you find these things, let us know and we'll work through them together. (Discord is great for that!)

 

Mostly, we'll be writing a lot of code. Set aside some time. Write out the examples in the book as you are reading. The time will be well spent. Related to that, some of the sample code is available as a download from the publisher. In general, I firmly believe that you'll get more out of typing the examples yourself than you ever will simply reviewing pre-written code. You won't learn to play the guitar by listening alone, you need to have your fingers on the strings. In much the same way, there are keyboard shortcuts that you pick up along the way, but experience requires trying things and making mistakes.

 

There are (online) exams every couple of weeks, and some projects to apply what you are reading about and practicing with sample code.

 

Speaking of online, the college lists this as a web course. You'll also hear it referred to as asynchronous. We won't have scheduled meetings, but we do have a class schedule to accomplish. The Canvas course is designed to guide you through, but we'll do it together, and I will make an effort to not let you feel alone through the process.

 

What to do next?

Find the course from your Canvas dashboard linked from the college website. Read through the front page material and take a look at the links that are there. I've tried to set it up so that all of the logical navigation links that you'll want are there. You can find out more about me, about the course, read the syllabus (there's a lot there) and then go to the first module and start reading the first three (3) chapters of the book.

 

Browse through the first chapter. Most of it will be review. If anything catches your interest, spend a few minutes reading about that, but you are welcome to simply browse through this material and maybe come back to it some other time. In Chapters 2 and 3, you'll get to write some code. I highly recommend that you start and end your reading sessions with the table of contents. Use this outline to find out what's in the chapter, and then as a quick review to remind yourself what you have read. The publisher's PowerPoint slides for the chapter are also in the module for this purpose as well.
