Migrating to the cloud: a business perspective

There is a lot of information on the internet and in literature about migrating businesses to the cloud, but what does that mean, and how relevant is it?

As we have seen, cloud technologies enable off-loading much of the infrastructure, installation, configuration, management and support of common computing services.

A business which plans to migrate essential functions to cloud services needs to consider the policies, procedures, and actions to ensure the availability of critical business functions to employees, customers, and other key stakeholders. Interruption to this core business continuity could be costly.

Migrating a business to the cloud can be a complex and challenging process. The scope and business case will influence the details. The process should consider all of these steps.

  1. Determine the Business Needs: Start by determining what the business needs from the cloud. Identify the business-critical applications and data that need to be migrated and evaluate the required cloud resources, including computing, storage, networking, and security.
  2. Choose a Cloud Service Provider: Research and evaluate different cloud service providers to determine which one best meets the business needs. Consider factors such as cost, reliability, security, and scalability. As you have seen, there may be a case for using services from more than one service provider. The added management of that approach should also be considered in the evaluation.
  3. Develop a Migration Plan: Develop a detailed migration plan that outlines the steps and timeline for the migration process. This plan should include a risk management strategy, a communication plan, and a plan for testing and validation.
  4. Migrate Data and Applications: Start by migrating non-critical applications and data to the cloud. This will allow the business to test the migration process and identify any potential issues or challenges. Once the non-critical applications and data have been successfully migrated, move on to the critical applications and data. Timing of switching between data sources also need to be carefully planned to avoid data loss. Database replication which creates two live copies on separate servers should be used. If one database fails, the other can immediately take over operations.
  5. Test and Validate: After the migration is complete, thoroughly test and validate the applications and data to ensure they are working correctly in the cloud environment.
  6. Optimize and Manage: Once the migration is complete, optimize the cloud resources and implement ongoing management and monitoring processes to ensure the cloud environment continues to meet the business needs.
  7. Train Staff: Train the staff to effectively use the new cloud environment and the associated tools and services.
  8. Monitor and Evaluate: Monitor the cloud environment regularly and evaluate the effectiveness of the migration. Make adjustments as needed to optimize the cloud environment and ensure it continues to meet the business needs.

Common Cloud Services

    Common Services include:
  • Computing Services, this includes serverless functions, hosted applications, and virtual computing platforms
  • Storage, for object storage, block storage, and file storage, and of course these are highly scalable, durable, and accessible
  • Networking Services, enabling creation and management of virtual networks, load balancers, firewalls, and other networking components
  • Database, enabling management, storage, and access to both relational and non-relational databases through either PaaS or user-managed IaaS solutions
  • Analytics and Big Data, providers offer services for analyzing large amounts of data, including machine learning, data warehousing, and business intelligence tools
  • Internet of Things (IOT), services for managing and analyzing data from IoT devices, such as sensors and connected devices
  • Security, various security services to help protect data and applications, including identity and access management, encryption, and threat detection.
  • Developer support offering a wide range of developer tools, including integrated development environments (IDEs), code repositories, and continuous integration and deployment (CI/CD) pipelines
  • Management and Monitoring cloud providers offer tools and services to help customers manage and monitor their cloud resources, including resource utilization, performance, and cost optimization

Business Applications

It's easy to see why cloud migration might start with a consideration of business needs. An established business should regularly review their operating needs and costs. As part of the planning discussions and activities, it should be recognized that migration of services doesn't have to be an all-at-once process.

Start with mapping business needs to cloud service offerings to determine which make sense and which are beneficial

A large defense contractor reviewing their IT usage requirements might choose to move email services away from internally managed servers to PaaS services, but may need to delay or take another approach to storage given consideration to export control concerns. Moving customer and internal proprietary data to cloud resources does not always allow for control over where the data is physically stored.

As time goes by, it becomes more common that building a cloud service foundation does not mean migrating existing infrastructure. The cloud service model creates opportunities for startups to create new products and services which would not have been possible just a few years ago.

A small accounting firm that I support was building their business and relying on a proprietary software package with a license model which allowed it to be installed on only a single CPU. In time, when the firm grows, they will reconsider their investment, but in the first year, the three partners, working in separate cities and time zones, decided to share scheduled access to a shared virtual machine. The VM's capabilities are scaled up during periods when performance requirements are at a peak, and scaled back when the VM is used less frequently. This resulted in a predictable and reasonable budget impact in their first year of operation.

Another startup I worked with, needed to provide a complex data integration app which could support thousands of concurrent users and share a database with a custom business analysis software package. The company created an online presence with continuous integration/ continuous deployment (CI/CD) product development, six separate database instances, and eventually integrated their accounting and billing processes with the online system. Real-time scaling of the cloud services enabled the company to predict and control costs while maintaining peak usage operations.

Whether migrating existing infrastucture, or building and growing new business, cloud services for large and small business are effecting very real change in the way that businesses operate.
