Print vs Online Media

I will preface this by humbly admitting that I have never written a book. I suppose I've co-authored papers in technical journals, but that's actually a stretch too. I worked in research labs and have been named as a contributor for papers and projects where I collected and processed data and helped with editing, but really I had very little to do with them.

Anyway, for discussion, this is only based on speculation and limited observation, I believe that if I sat down to author a textbook, developed and illustrated the content, went through more than a few back-and-forth cycleswith editors and peer reviews, when my textbook finally gets to first printing, and selected for classroom use by an instructor, and ordered by the school's bookstore, and purchased by the first group of students, and whatever other hoops have been cleared, considerable time has passed from when the content was first created and first consumed by the reader. In many, and in fact most cases, this is not a bad thing. I suspect that not a lot has changed in basic Calculus or Byzantine history, although perhaps new perspectives and teaching approaches warrant new textbooks.

I am certainly not against textbooks. I have many, some are excellent, and I'm grateful to the subject matter experts who took the time to organize concepts and shared them in print.

Recency is a point on the plus side of internet search, but of course it also carries the risk of unverified sources. As you know, you can't believe everything you read.

I just finished reading through your exploration of the current state of cloud service providers and I was encouraged that one thing that you discovered in this changing landscape, is that there is in fact, change.

I was very happy to read your discussions and to see you taking a critical interest in not just what your findings are, but also the sources. I hope that you found it as interesting as I did.

 

Amazon Web Services

Yes, I've written in your Canvas notes some information about how they arrived and not just that they are currently the largest player in the market, and also the first, but in my opinion, why. I invite you to research the background on many of these and form your own opinions.

 

Microsoft Azure

Some of you looked at available data and found that, while MS has been in this #2 seat for a while, they have been closing the gap between them and AWS in recent years. In sports and in business, you know that the change in relative position and the rate of change is as important as the position. It's good that your horse is in front, but do you see how fast the next horse is catching up? Microsoft has been investing in, and growing their cloud infrastructure, as well as their marketing. It is not surprising that they are positioned as well as they are.

On a side note, although I am trying to present a level field and honest comparison, I do have more professional cloud experience with Microsoft than the others, including for clients and personal use.

 

The "others"

Having seen the discrepancy in market-share between the top 2 and perhaps 3, the plan to have you look at the nominal top 10 was deliberate. I will call this nominal because it was hardly scientific or even heavily statistical. Still, your survey(s) pointed out some interesting findings.

I knew that using just about any source, the top 3 would be clear. It may surprise you to hear that when I conducted this same survey 5 years ago, it was IBM in the number 3 position. But there was more to discover in these other major providers.

 

Alibaba is one of the largest import/export, e-commerce, and banking companies in the world, to have such a strong footprint in this market is no surprise, I would have expected to see them ranked higher. There were big corrections in the global economy last 2 years when China began making efforts to break up some of the large monopolies, and stock prices fell. It would not surprise me to see them recover and grow faster in coming years.

Google Cloud also isn't too big a surprise, but again the change in market share is more interesting than the standing, having been a relatively smaller presence in the past, it is now a main focal point for parent company Alphabet.

 

Yes, there are many players, large and small. We will be looking more at the fundamental technologies and architectures of the generic cloud, but now that we have established these big 3 as the industry leaders, that is where we will focus.
