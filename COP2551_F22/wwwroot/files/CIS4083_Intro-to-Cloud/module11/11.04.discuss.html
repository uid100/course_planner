What's Next?

The future's so bright, you gotta wear shades! -- Timbuk 3

A colleague of mine shared some statistics at the beginning of this semester which indicated surprisingly (to me) pessimistic student hope for the future.

When I look back at my own experience at or near the end of my undergraduate studies, I remember feeling apprehension and uncertainty for what was to come, so it really shouldn't have been all that surprising.

In my case, I was recently married, my wife and I were both launching into our new life with brand new college degrees, no clear paths for careers, and a pretty good network of similarly unemployed and under-employed friends.

The world was politically unstable, global military threats were growing, nationwide health crises and looming declarations of war were nightly news, unemployment was rising, economic recession was reality, housing costs were at all-time crisis levels, and we had very little idea of what was next.

Sound familiar?

These observations could have been in the 40s, the 60s, the 80s, or just about any time since then, including today. So none of these statistics and observations should have surprised me. Perhaps I had simply forgotten.

We were able to get jobs to (just) pay the bills, we continued to expand our circle of friends, and we jumped (for better or worse) when the (lucky) breaks came our way. We weren't particularly patient or hope-filled, but we just kept going and made decisions along the way that we hoped were right, or at least not too wrong. You know, living.

Along the way was a series of experiences and decisions and occurrences that all added up to be a terrific story, but one we could not have planned and could not have predicted.

This week, I want to share three more perspectives for you to consider and compare. All three of these were recorded about ten years ago, prior to the global Covid-19 pandemic and all have a common optistic view of possibilities, and somewhere in here, maybe is the key to the question, What's next?

Marco Annunziata is an economist who discusses what he calls a metamorphic change. and the age of the industrial internet.

According to his hypotheses, what will be the impact on jobs as a result?

Carl Bass from Autodesk references the power of Infinite Computing as a factor in modern technical advances with specific references. He challenges some of the traditional limitations of having large teams of support and access to investment capital, and in contrast, emphasizes diversity and mindset.

This is consistent with one of my favorite depictions of diversity, specifically, the collaboration of dissimilar perspectives and sharing of ideas from different backgrounds.

With the topics we have explored, using cloud resources to support computing tasks, virtualization of compute platforms and networks, access to data without practical limits, data analytics, sensors and control systems, management, and even collaboration with human resources, from both an historic and current-state perspective, do you agree with the notion that we have unprecedented access to infinite computing? What, if any are the limitations? Is this trending in one direction or another?

Along the subject of what is possible, Peter Diamandis provides statistics which counter the popular media emphasis on the negative. It is a message of optimism and a reminder that the next life-saving, hope-giving, community-changing, fortune-making ideas are all around us and their source is within reach. This message was delivered ten years ago, but he asserts that technology and capabilities are continuing to increase at a trajectory consistent with Moore's Law (doubling every 12 to 24 months).

Rather than dismissing the very real problems facing society now and throughout history, he presents specific approaches to advance toward solutions.

Access to cloud technologies opens possibilities, but possibilities still require innovation. Global problems exist and many of them have existed since the dawn of mankind in some form or other. Experience shows us time and time again that solutions come incrementally. Amazing super-computing power from the 1970s taken out of context would easily be dismissed as simplistic compared with the mobile device we all carry everyday. Even the flip-phones that you probably had once were once considered technical marvels and obsolete recycling problems today. The solutions to tomorrow's problems are no different. They won't come all at once, but in small steps. Be watchful for the tiny forward steps, and the limitless resources at hand for developing those ideas today.
