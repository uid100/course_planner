Authentication and Authorization

  • There is a sign near the entrance to rides at amusement parks which indicate a minimum height requirement for safety purposes.
  • A guard stands by the backstage door at a music venue checking passes.
  • Sentries are posted at the gates of a military installation carefully inspecting identification credentials.
  • The cashier at the corner store is required to confirm your government-issued identification before selling you age-restricted products.
  • An RFID card reader operates the locks on the doors to corporate offices.

All of these are intended to legitimately and conscientiously discriminate against unauthorized access.

Additionally, if you are building or managing an online application or system, it is likely that there are some activities that only specific users should be allowed to access.

But in order to control this gate or filter to allow some users in and keeping others out, you need a way to identify them.

The process of allowing qualified access is called authorization.

Confidently identifying or qualifying the user is authentication.

Authorization

Allowing and disallowing user permissions may be granted at the individual or group (role) level. Granting a particular user all of the permissions they may require for a particular job can be tedious in large organizations, so it's often easier to assign them to a particular role that has a permission.

All users should be able to access their own messages, but not messages intended for others. Elevated permissions are usually granted to system administrators, who may need to add and delete users and roles.

Authentication

How do I know it's you?

Authentication, if not most important, becomes very important. This is not a new concept for application development, but you might be surprised at how the solutions and performance expectations have changed over the years.

The question eventually becomes how to implement user authentication.

Once upon a time, it was enough to prompt the user and ask for their username. (Ok. Hi Bob. Welcome Back.) If the user had physical access to the system, it must be ok, and all I really care about is what their name is, so I know how to greet them and where to find their files.

That was adequate until it wasn't. Next the application would ask for a password and the application would store the password. In time, this became bad practice too.

How many passwords do we need to manage now? If we are using strong encryption to manage and encrypt store and recover passwords, there are many points of failure and overhead and risk.

In most cases, I don't need to know anything about you except to recognize you when you return. I likely don't need to know anything about the user, except that they were here before and that someone else isn't pretending to be them.

Software libraries were written to help with this, and we could build databases and support methods, but the result is reinventing a wheel which is probably already obsolete.

Federated passwords and IDaaS is a much better solution.

Perhaps not 5 years ago, but by now, most of us are familiar with Single Sign-On (SSO) and multi-factor authentication (MFA).

When you visit sites that invite you to login using Facebook or Google or Microsoft, they very likely do not care about your Facebook or Google or Microsoft accounts, they are simply using them as a trusted authority to validate the claim that you are who you say your are. When you log in through these systems, they will issue a credential to you and vouch for you to the requesting system. They don't share any other information other than "yes, I know this user."

As developers, we probably don't care about where a user had dinner or which memes they LOL-d. All we care about is that a trusted authority said, "yeah, he is who he claims to be. (Because I said so.)"
